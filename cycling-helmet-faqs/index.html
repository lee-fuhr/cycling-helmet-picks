Cycling Helmet questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A certified cycling helmet with a secure fit is the single most important piece of safety gear for any rider. While features like MIPS can add rotational impact protection, the best helmet is one that fits you well, is comfortable enough to wear every ride, and meets current safety standards (CPSC, EN 1078, or AS/NZS 2063).

What safety certifications matter for a cycling helmet?

The minimum you should look for is a helmet that meets the CPSC standard (in the U.S.) or EN 1078 (in Europe) or AS/NZS 2063 (in Australia). These certifications ensure the helmet can absorb the energy of a single severe impact. No helmet on the market can protect against all crash scenarios, but a certified helmet has passed rigorous drop tests. MIPS (Multi-directional Impact Protection System) is an additional layer that reduces rotational forces in an angled impact. It’s not a requirement, but many riders choose MIPS for an extra margin of safety. Budget-friendly helmets without MIPS are still very protective; the difference is marginal for most everyday crashes.

When do I need to replace my cycling helmet?

Replace your helmet immediately after any crash or impact, even if you see no cracks. The EPS foam inside compresses and may not protect again. Most manufacturers recommend replacing a helmet every three to five years, even without a crash, because UV light, sweat, and temperature changes degrade the foam over time. If you notice any frayed straps, loose padding, or a cracked shell, that’s a sign to replace it. Commuters who ride daily in sun or rain might need a new helmet sooner than someone who rides occasionally in a garage.

How should a bicycle helmet fit?

A helmet should sit level on your head, not tilted back, with the front edge about two finger widths above your eyebrows. The straps should form a “V” just below your ears, and the chin strap should be snug enough that you can only fit one finger between it and your chin. Shake your head vigorously; the helmet should not move more than a centimeter in any direction. Many helmets come with adjustable sizing rings or interchangeable pads to dial in the fit. A helmet that feels loose when you ride can shift and expose your forehead or temples. It’s worth spending time in a store trying on different brands, because shapes vary, oval versus round heads.

Road vs. mountain bike helmets: what’s the difference?

Road helmets are designed for ventilation and aerodynamics at speed. They tend to be lighter, with more vents facing forward, and often have a teardrop shape. A road helmet may offer less rear coverage than a mountain bike helmet because you’re less likely to land on the back of your head on pavement. Mountain bike helmets prioritize coverage, especially around the back and sides, and often include a fixed visor to block sun and brush. Some also have a lower rear lip to protect against strikes from branches. Many commuter helmets blend features: moderate coverage, a simple visor, and the ability to mount a light. Choose based on where you ride most.

Does spending more on a helmet make you safer?

All certified helmets meet the same basic impact standard, so a budget-friendly model will protect you just as well as a top-of-the-line racer in a direct hit. The extra cost goes toward weight reduction, ventilation, aerodynamics, comfort features (like a fine-tune fit dial or sweat-wicking pads), and sometimes a premium finish. If you can afford a helmet that feels more comfortable, you’ll be more likely to wear it consistently, and that’s what really improves safety. That said, some expensive models incorporate MIPS or other rotational impact tech, which may reduce the risk of concussion in angled crashes. But a hundred-dollar helmet with MIPS can be just as protective as a three-hundred-dollar one with MIPS minus the weight savings. Our advice: buy the best-fitting helmet you can, then consider MIPS if it fits your budget.

What should I look for in a helmet for bike commuting?

Visibility and convenience matter as much as fit. Look for a helmet that has a built-in rear light or mounts for one, bright colors or reflectivity, and enough coverage to protect against low-hanging branches or a fall over the handlebars. A visor helps with rain and sun, and a removable, washable liner is a plus for daily use. Commuting helmets often trade extreme lightness for reasonable weight and added features. Some models include an integrated flip-down visor or a “ponytail” port for long hair. You don’t need the most aerodynamic shape; focus on fit, ventilation during stop-and-go traffic, and how easy it is to carry when you lock up your bike.

Frequently asked questions

Are cheaper bike helmets safe?

Yes, as long as they carry a recognized safety certification like CPSC or EN 1078. Inexpensive helmets often lack MIPS or premium ventilation, but they pass the same impact tests. The risk is that a very cheap helmet might fit poorly, which can reduce its effectiveness.

Can I use a bike helmet for skateboarding or climbing?

No. Bike helmets are tested for single, high-speed impacts typical of cycling. Skateboard and climbing helmets have different certification standards (like ASTM F1492) for multiple lower-speed impacts or special shell shapes. Use the right helmet for each sport.

Do I really need MIPS?

MIPS reduces rotational forces in angled impacts, which can lower the risk of brain injury. It’s not mandatory, but many riders choose it for peace of mind. For most commuting and recreational riding, a non-MIPS certified helmet is still very protective.

How do I properly clean my cycling helmet?

Wash the pads and straps with mild soap and warm water, then air-dry them. Avoid harsh chemicals, solvents, or hot water. The outer shell can be wiped with a damp cloth. Never submerge the foam inner shell. Replace pads when they get flat or smelly.

What’s the best way to store a bike helmet?

Store it in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and extreme heat (like a car trunk in summer). Don’t hang it on a mirror or hook by the straps, that can stretch them. Keep it in a helmet bag or on a shelf. Avoid stacking heavy objects on top.

Do aero helmets make a real difference?

For competitive racers at high speeds, a well-designed aero helmet can save a few seconds over 40 km (25 miles). For most recreational riders or commuters, the aerodynamic benefit is negligible, and the reduced ventilation can be uncomfortable. Choose an aero helmet only if you ride fast and prioritize speed over comfort.

In shortThe helmet that will keep you safest is the one you actually wear, and wear correctly. Fit should be your first concern, then safety certification, then features like MIPS or a visor. Replace it after a crash or every few years, and store it gently. Whether you spend a little or a lot, a well-fitted, certified helmet is the best insurance you can buy for every ride.
